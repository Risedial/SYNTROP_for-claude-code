# Tagline Options

> **Source of Truth:** `metadata/system-facts.json`
> **Usage:** These are candidates, not finals. Select one primary tagline and optionally one secondary/sub-tagline. Evaluate against your chosen brand name — the pairing matters.

---

## Evaluation Criteria

A strong tagline for SYNTROP should:
- Be understood by a developer on first read, without context
- Describe what the system does OR the problem it solves — not a vague aspiration
- Be honest (no "build anything," no "the future of")
- Work at the scale it will be read: GitHub README one-liner, Product Hunt tagline, Twitter bio

---

## Category 1: Problem-First Taglines

These lead with the problem the system solves. Strong for audiences who recognize the pain.

---

**1. "The context window ends. The project doesn't."**

**Rationale:** Names the exact problem (context window death) and immediately resolves it. Technical, precise, and memorable as a one-liner. Works well for Hacker News and developer audiences who have felt this pain.

**Use when:** Speaking to developers who've used Claude Code for multi-session projects and hit the wall.

**Risk:** Slightly technical — non-Claude users may not immediately parse "context window."

---

**2. "AI that remembers what it's building."**

**Rationale:** Translates the technical innovation (state machine persistence) into a human-readable benefit. Contrast with other AI tools that don't remember. Simple enough for non-developers while remaining accurate.

**Use when:** Broader audience, landing page hero, social posts.

**Risk:** "AI that remembers" is a functional description, not a differentiating category claim.

---

**3. "Your AI development process — minus the amnesia."**

**Rationale:** Slightly playful but precise. "Amnesia" is the right word for what happens when a session ends. Humanizes the technical problem without losing accuracy.

**Use when:** Community posts, Twitter threads, product launch.

**Risk:** The word "amnesia" may feel too casual for some audiences.

---

**4. "When one session isn't enough."**

**Rationale:** Honest framing — this is explicitly for projects that don't fit in a single conversation. Self-selects the right user. Understated in the best way.

**Use when:** Opening line in documentation, sidebar positioning statement.

**Risk:** Less memorable as a standalone tagline.

---

## Category 2: Outcome-First Taglines

These lead with what you get. Strong for audiences motivated by output quality.

---

**5. "Brain dump in. Deployment-ready out."**

**Rationale:** Describes the full arc of the system in 5 words. "Brain dump" normalizes starting messy. "Deployment-ready" sets the quality bar. Concrete and verifiable.

**Use when:** Product Hunt tagline, GitHub description, landing page hero secondary line.

**Risk:** May set expectations that the system does all the work (it doesn't — multiple sessions required). Pair with a limitation acknowledgment nearby.

---

**6. "From raw idea to complete project. Across unlimited sessions."**

**Rationale:** Full claim, no ambiguity. Describes exactly what the system does. The "unlimited sessions" differentiator is explicit.

**Use when:** Longer format — README opening paragraph, landing page feature section header.

**Risk:** Too long for a Twitter bio or Product Hunt tagline.

---

**7. "Ship projects. Not sessions."**

**Rationale:** Tight contrast. Other tools help within a session. SYNTROP delivers complete projects. Plays on "ship" (deploy) and "session" (the unit of AI work).

**Use when:** Any context where brevity matters. Great as a secondary tagline paired with a longer one.

**Risk:** "Ship" is developer-culture speak — may need translating for less technical audiences.

---

**8. "33 skills. 5 phases. One command."**

**Rationale:** Factual, precise, and does the work of explaining the architecture in 7 words. Developers who like systems will immediately want to know what the 33 skills are.

**Use when:** Technical audience, Product Hunt, GitHub topic line.

**Risk:** Numerical — if the architecture changes (more workers added), needs updating.

---

## Category 3: Architecture-First Taglines

These lead with the structural innovation. Strong for technically sophisticated audiences.

---

**9. "Multi-session AI project orchestration, finally solved."**

**Rationale:** Names the category and claims to solve it. "Finally" is a loaded word — only works if the audience has been frustrated by the lack of this. Strong for Hacker News where the framing matches developer experience.

**Use when:** Show HN post title, HN comments, technical forums.

**Risk:** "Finally solved" is a large claim for a v1.0. Pair with honest acknowledgment of limitations.

---

**10. "A CEO, 5 directors, 23 workers. For your side project."**

**Rationale:** Unexpected and precise. The architecture sounds impressive when stated this way — and it's completely true. "For your side project" grounds it and prevents grandiosity.

**Use when:** Twitter/X threads, technical introductions, "what is this?" explanations.

**Risk:** May confuse people who think it involves actual people. Needs a one-sentence follow-up.

---

**11. "Structure for AI development that spans sessions."**

**Rationale:** Pure positioning statement. No hype, just function. The kind of thing that goes in a technical specification or architecture document.

**Use when:** README description, architecture docs, contributor-facing materials.

**Risk:** Not memorable enough for consumer-facing marketing.

---

## Category 4: Control-First Taglines

These emphasize user control — the human approval gates.

---

**12. "Autonomous execution. Human decisions."**

**Rationale:** The two-part structure captures the exact balance. The system runs autonomously within phases. Humans approve at phase gates. This is a real differentiator from "fire and hope" tools.

**Use when:** Positioning against Devin/Replit/Bolt.new in comparison contexts.

**Risk:** Generic without context — could describe many human-in-the-loop systems.

---

**13. "You're still the architect. It's just doing the construction."**

**Rationale:** The metaphor is apt. Developers make design decisions (architecture review → Research phase approval, blueprint review → Architecture phase approval). The system executes. This framing respects developer expertise rather than promising to replace it.

**Use when:** Longer marketing copy, explainer content, onboarding.

**Risk:** Too long for a tagline. Better as a section headline or explainer quote.

---

**14. "The project lives in a file. Not in a session."**

**Rationale:** Precise technical claim that is immediately understandable and immediately differentiated. The state file is the innovation. Sessions end; the file persists.

**Use when:** Technical audiences. HN, developer blogs.

**Risk:** Abstract for general audiences.

---

**15. "Open-source. Local. Stateful. Yours."**

**Rationale:** Four one-word differentiators that address the main objections to AI coding tools (cloud lock-in, data privacy, context loss, ownership). Honest. Technically accurate.

**Use when:** Trust-building contexts — addressing skeptics, comparing against cloud-hosted tools.

**Risk:** Telegraphic — reads like a features list, not a story.

---

## Pairing Recommendations

**For a landing page:** Lead with #5 ("Brain dump in. Deployment-ready out."), follow with #7 ("Ship projects. Not sessions.") as secondary.

**For GitHub description:** #8 ("33 skills. 5 phases. One command.") — precise and factual.

**For Hacker News Show HN:** #9 ("Multi-session AI project orchestration, finally solved.") — respects the audience.

**For Twitter/X bio:** #2 ("AI that remembers what it's building.") — accessible, differentiating.

**For Product Hunt tagline:** #5 or #7 — outcome-focused, brief.

**For technical documentation:** #11 ("Structure for AI development that spans sessions.") — no hype.
